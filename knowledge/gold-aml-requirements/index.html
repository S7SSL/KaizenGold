Gold AML Requirements Explained

Anti-money laundering (AML) requirements oblige participants in the gold trade to verify who they deal with, understand the source of metal and funds, monitor transactions for suspicious patterns, report suspicions to authorities, and keep records — because gold's high value, portability and near-cash liquidity make it a recognised vehicle for laundering criminal proceeds. Dealers in precious metals are regulated entities for AML purposes in most major jurisdictions, and banks will not finance gold flows that lack demonstrable AML controls.

Why Gold Attracts Laundering — and Regulators

Gold concentrates enormous value in small, anonymous, globally saleable form. A kilogram fits in a hand and converts to currency in any major city. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) has long identified dealing in precious metals as a higher-risk activity, and national regimes implement that view: dealers above thresholds are typically regulated alongside financial institutions, with obligations modelled on banking AML rules.

The Core Obligations

  1. Customer due diligence (CDD). Identify and verify customers and their beneficial owners before transacting — the KYC process. Enhanced due diligence applies to higher-risk customers: politically exposed persons, complex structures, and counterparties connected to higher-risk jurisdictions.
  2. Source of funds and source of metal. Gold AML is two-directional: the buyer's money and the seller's metal both need legitimate, evidenced origins. Origin documentation and chain of custody are the metal-side counterpart to bank-side source-of-funds checks.
  3. Transaction monitoring. Watch for patterns inconsistent with a customer's profile: structuring below thresholds, rapid in-and-out flows, pricing that makes no commercial sense, or counterparties that change mid-transaction.
  4. Suspicious activity reporting. Where suspicion arises, regulated dealers must report to their national financial intelligence unit and must not tip off the customer.
  5. Record-keeping. Identification documents, transaction records and supporting documentation retained for the statutory period (commonly five years or more).
  6. Governance. A written risk assessment, appointed compliance responsibility, staff training and independent review — proportionate to the business's size and risk.

How This Plays Out in a Physical Gold Deal

Before any instrument is issued or metal moves, the parties exchange and verify: corporate registrations, beneficial ownership declarations, licences (mining, export, dealing), the metal's origin documentation, and sanctions screening results for every party and bank in the chain — see Sanctions Compliance for Precious Metals. Banks issuing letters of credit run their own AML review of the transaction; a deal that cannot satisfy a bank's compliance desk will not get an instrument, whatever its commercial merits.

Example

A refinery's trading arm is offered 20 kg of doré monthly from a new exporter. AML review covers: the exporter's licence and corporate documents; beneficial owners screened against sanctions and PEP lists; mine source records evidencing the metal's origin; consistency between declared volumes and the source mine's plausible production; and the buyer-side bank's own review before issuing the credit. Two months in, offered volumes suddenly triple with no new source documentation — a monitoring red flag that triggers enhanced review before any further shipment is accepted.

Red Flags Recognised Across the Industry

  • Metal offered significantly below the world price — legitimate gold sells at the benchmark.
  • Origin documentation missing, inconsistent, or from a different country than claimed.
  • Counterparties unwilling to complete KYC, or substituting parties mid-deal.
  • Requests for cash settlement, third-party payments, or split payments to unrelated accounts.
  • Volumes implausible for the claimed source.

AML compliance is not an obstacle to gold trading — it is the licence to participate. Counterparties with strong controls clear banks, refineries and customs faster, and command better terms.

Key Takeaways

  • Gold's value density and liquidity make it a recognised money-laundering vehicle, so dealers are AML-regulated in most major jurisdictions.
  • Core duties: customer due diligence, source-of-funds and source-of-metal checks, monitoring, suspicious activity reporting and record-keeping.
  • Gold AML is two-directional — the buyer's funds and the seller's metal both need evidenced legitimate origin.
  • Banks apply their own AML review before issuing instruments; non-compliant deals don't get financed.
  • Below-market pricing, missing origin papers and KYC reluctance are red flags the entire industry recognises.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who regulates AML in gold trading?

National authorities implementing FATF-derived standards — for example HMRC supervises high-value dealers in the UK, FinCEN rules cover US dealers in precious metals, and UAE authorities regulate the sector domestically including through DMCC rules for member firms. The applicable regime depends on where each party operates.

Is there a cash limit for buying gold?

Many jurisdictions restrict or report large cash transactions in precious metals — thresholds vary by country. Professional trade is conducted through banking channels precisely to avoid the risks cash creates.

What is enhanced due diligence?

Deeper verification applied to higher-risk situations: politically exposed persons, complex ownership structures, higher-risk jurisdictions or unusual transaction patterns — more documentation, senior approval and closer monitoring.

What happens if a dealer ignores AML obligations?

Regulatory penalties, criminal liability in serious cases, loss of banking relationships, and exclusion from the legitimate supply chain — refineries and banks drop counterparties that create compliance exposure.

Does AML apply to one-off private sales?

Regulated obligations attach mainly to businesses dealing in precious metals. But banks and dealers on the other side of a private sale will still apply their own checks to the funds and the metal.
